Glass Days 2024

Understanding glass cuttting and glass in the Gallic period

02–05 April 2024

A week dedicated to glass

Glass is a remarkable material that has accompanied human history for more than 10,000 years. Today, it is also a key material for achieving the energy transition.

Where does glass come from? How is it manufactured? How can we reduce its carbon footprint? These questions were explored during Glass Days 2024 at Lycée Jean Monnet in Yzeure.

The third edition of this educational event gathered students, teachers, artists, historians, engineers and researchers around a shared passion: glass.

This year, participants reconstructed a Gallic furnace and explored how glass was produced and shaped 2,000 years ago. They also experimented with glass cutting, polishing, colouring and the production of glass reproducing the compositions of stained-glass windows.

The programme combined morning lectures involving all glass-training programmes (CAP, BMA, DNMADe…) with afternoon workshops where students, teachers and artists worked together to understand glass technologies, develop new CO₂-free glass compositions and explore historical glass-making techniques.

Experimental archaeology: reconstruction of a Gallic furnace for glass production

The reconstruction of a Gallic furnace allowed participants to investigate ancient melting and glass-making techniques through experimental archaeology.

Students explored how raw materials were transformed into glass and how ancient craftsmen controlled temperature, viscosity and glass shaping processes.

The construction of the furnace also provided an opportunity to integrate temperature and oxygen fugacity sensors. These measurements made it possible to determine experimentally the temperature distribution at different locations within the furnace, as well as the degree to which the atmosphere was oxidizing or reducing. This information is essential for understanding the redox processes occurring inside the furnace, which strongly influence the physicochemical properties of the glass, including its colour.

The data collected during the experiment were analysed and presented to the students at the end of the week, allowing them to compare the experimental measurements with archaeological hypotheses and to better understand the relationship between furnace operating conditions and the properties of the produced glass.
