ANR 3VER

Towards Greener Glass

A collaborative research project, funded by the French National Research Agency (ANR), bringing together researchers, teachers, students, glass artists, and craftspeople to imagine and develop the next generation of sustainable glasses.

The project is inspired by the annual Glass Day, organized jointly by the Union for Glass Science and Technology (USTV) and the Lycée Jean Monnet – École Nationale du Verre.

Project Overview

Glass is an exceptionally versatile material that has been part of human life for thousands of years. It will also play a key role in future carbon-neutral technologies. However, current glass production remains highly energy-intensive and generates significant CO₂ emissions.

Achieving a carbon-neutral glass industry by the middle of the 21st century requires a fundamental transformation of both raw materials and melting technologies. Today, most glass — from industrial production to artisanal manufacturing — is produced in gas-fired furnaces, which emit large quantities of CO₂. These furnaces remain widely used because they offer great flexibility, allowing precise control of production rates, temperature profiles and redox conditions, all of which strongly influence the properties of the final glass.

Reducing greenhouse gas emissions therefore requires the development of alternative solutions, including low-carbon raw materials and new melting technologies. Several approaches are already being explored, such as hydrogen-assisted combustion, electric furnaces and the increased use of recycled glass (cullet). Among these solutions, recycling has already become widespread in the glass industry.

A new pathway: volcanic resources for greener glass

The 3VER project explores an innovative approach based on the combination of recycled glass with volcanic lava. By replacing up to 50 wt.% of conventional raw materials, volcanic materials could eliminate the need for carbonate-based components, which are among the main sources of CO₂ emissions during glass production.

Combined with recycled glass, these natural resources could significantly reduce both the environmental impact and the energy consumption of the melting process.

A major scientific challenge is to maintain suitable high-temperature working properties while preserving the physical and chemical characteristics required for glass processing. These constraints apply equally to ancient glassmakers, modern artisans and industrial production at the tonne scale. Operations such as gathering, blowing, shaping and tempering rely on the same fundamental principles and are governed by the high-temperature behaviour of the glass.

From laboratory research to industrial applications

The 3VER project aims to develop new glass formulations designed for low-carbon production of everyday objects using electric furnaces. These innovative glasses will combine recycled glass (cullet) with different types and proportions of volcanic lava, avoiding the use of carbonate-based raw materials.

Project objectives

  • Develop and characterise new sustainable glass compositions.
  • Adapt these formulations to different glass-processing techniques.
  • Optimise their production in electric furnaces.
  • Transfer laboratory-scale research towards industrial applications.

Beyond the development of new glass compositions, 3VER also aims to prepare future generations of glassmakers for these innovative materials and future technological transitions in the glass industry. Research on new formulations and their adaptation to traditional glassmaking practices will be carried out collaboratively by all project partners, with strong involvement from teachers and students of Lycée Jean Monnet – École Nationale du Verre.
